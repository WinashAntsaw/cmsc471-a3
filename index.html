White Hat and Black Hat Visualizaitons of Greenhouse Gas Data

To create this black hat chart, we focused on average per-capita greenhouse gas emissions for the US, China, and India but never explicitly label it as “per capita.” While the US does have higher per-person emissions, China’s total is much larger, with 12 million tons versus the US’s 4 million. That difference is masked when divided by China’s massive population. India, with 2 million total but a far larger population, appears negligible on a per-capita basis, reinforcing the illusion that the US is the worst offender. We also slightly truncate the y-axis to start at 1 instead of 0, exaggerating differences among the bars. Cutting it further would magnify this gap even more, but we are limited by India’s already tiny bar. Another deceptive tactic is filtering out post-2016 data. China and India’s observations end before 2016, while the US has downward-trending years after that. Ignoring these later points increases the US’s average. Meanwhile, we aggregate “average emissions,” knowing the US has far more data points than India and China, further skewing comparisons. By cherry-picking these three nations, we omit countries like Russia, Mexico, or Canada, whose populations and emissions might diminish the US’s apparent lead. Finally, applying a color scale and coloring the US with a disproportionately red color visually amplifies its size. All together, omitting “per capita” from the chart (labeling), truncating the y-axis and using a deceptive color scale (encoding), cutting off later data, and selecting just three countries (data transformation), this creates an effective black hat visualization that falsely suggests the US is by far the world’s largest emitter.
